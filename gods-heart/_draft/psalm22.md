## 15 My mouth[d] is dried up like a potsherd,
- Mouth dried up like a potsherd: This vivid metaphor illustrates extreme thirst and dehydration. A potsherd is a broken piece of pottery, which, when dried out, becomes brittle and cracked. in medical terms this could indicate severe dehydration, possibly due to conditions like **hypovolemia** (a significant loss of body fluids) . this can be caused by factors such as prolonged fasting, severe diarrhea, or excessive sweating without adequate fluid replacement. The imagery of a dried potsherd effectively conveys the psalmist's intense physical suffering and desperation for relief. Dr. Alexander Metherell book about crucifixion: "The dehydration resulting from crucifixion would lead to a parched mouth and throat, making it difficult for the victim to speak or cry out for help. This physical state of extreme thirst is poignantly captured in [Psalm 22:15](https://www.bible.com/bible/59/PSA.22.15.ESV), where the psalmist describes his mouth as being 'dried up like a potsherd,' highlighting the severity of his suffering and the desperate need for relief." (Source: Metherell, Alexander. *The Crucifixion: Understanding the Physical Trauma of Christ's Death*. 2014.)

The Hebrew word "worm" in [Psalm 22:6](https://www.bible.com/bible/59/PSA.22.6.ESV) means "crimson worm" or "scarlet worm" and refers to a type of insect that was used in ancient times to produce a red dye. This imagery is significant because it symbolizes suffering and humiliation, as the psalmist describes himself as being "scorned by mankind and despised by the people." The reference to the "worm" highlights the depth of the psalmist's anguish and his sense of being utterly forsaken.

**Key Observations:**

1. **Prophetic Precision**: Both Isaiah 53 and Psalm 22 contain remarkably specific details that were fulfilled literally in Christ's crucifixion, written centuries before crucifixion was even invented by the Romans.

2. **Complementary Perspectives**: 
   - Isaiah 53 focuses more on the **theological significance** (substitutionary atonement)
   - Psalm 22 provides more **physical and emotional details** of the suffering

3. **Historical Context**: Psalm 22 was likely written by David around 1000 BC, and Isaiah 53 around 700 BC, both predicting details of Roman crucifixion methods that wouldn't be developed until centuries later.

4. **Jesus' Identification**: By quoting Psalm 22:1 on the cross, Jesus was directing people to read the entire psalm, which moves from suffering (vv. 1-21) to victory and worldwide worship (vv. 22-31).
